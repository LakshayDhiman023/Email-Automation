Study 01 · your question

Gmail inside the app — "land on gmail.com within our website"

"Can we add a view-gmail option, well within our website (if it's legal)?"
REJECT iframe · BUILD deep links

The hard fact (verified today)

Gmail's servers answer with X-Frame-Options: SAMEORIGIN — the browser itself refuses to render gmail.com inside any other site's frame. This isn't a legal grey zone we could risk; it's a technical wall. Every workaround (proxying Google's pages, stripping headers) violates Google's ToS and would trip their anti-abuse systems → account suspension risk, the one thing P4 exists to prevent.

What similar tools do

Streak, Mixmax, Boomerang went the other direction — they inject INTO Gmail as browser extensions. Superhuman/Missive rebuilt an entire mail client on the API. Nobody embeds gmail.com, because nobody can.

The salvage — 90% of the value at 2% of the cost

We already store gmail_thread_id for every thread. Gmail supports direct URLs to a thread:

mail.google.com/mail/u/0/#all/<thread_id>
  • "Open in Gmail ↗" button on every thread card (Pipeline, Replies, Sent log)
  • Lands on the EXACT conversation, not the inbox
  • Zero new permissions, zero ToS exposure, ~20 lines of code

Pairs with parked Feature 04 (inline reply reading): read in-app for speed, jump to Gmail for the full client when you need to reply manually.

Verdict: embedding is impossible by Google's own headers — killing this idea is a calculation, not an opinion. Deep links deliver the actual need ("get me to this conversation fast") for half a day of work. Recommend: BUILD deep links.
Study 02 · your question

Calendly-style meeting booking

"See if we can add a feature like Calendly to book meetings."
REJECT rebuild · BUILD integration

The math on rebuilding it

  • Needs a PUBLIC booking page → but Mailflow has no public-facing auth story (login is deliberately deferred). New attack surface on day one.
  • Availability model, double-booking locks, timezone math for the INVITEE, reschedule/cancel flows, calendar invites (.ics), reminder emails… that's a second product, not a feature.
  • Google Calendar API is free, but this is ~3-4 weeks of work to reach "worse than Calendly's free tier".

The decisive fact

Cal.com is open-source and self-hostable — the exact ethos as Mailflow (your data, $0, own deployment). Calendly's free tier also works. Rebuilding what the ecosystem already gives away free fails our own "evaluate before build" test.

What we build instead (the 1/5-effort version)

  • Settings → "Booking link" field (your Cal.com / Calendly URL)
  • {booking_link} becomes a built-in variable like {signature} — usable in any template or follow-up
  • "Send booking link" quick action on positive replies: one click drafts a courteous reply with your link, pre-filled into the existing approval flow (P3 intact — you still approve it)
  • Onboarding checklist can later gain an optional "add a booking link" step

Result: the recruiter books a meeting in YOUR calendar from OUR email — which is the outcome you actually wanted, without owning a scheduling engine.

Verdict: booking the meeting matters; owning the booking engine doesn't. Recommend: BUILD the {booking_link} integration, point users at Cal.com/Calendly free. Revisit a native "suggest 3 free slots" (Google Calendar free/busy) only if the link flow proves too weak in practice.
Study 03 · from similar tools

Email verification before sending (MX check)

What NeverBounce/ZeroBounce charge for, the free-tier slice of it.
BUILD

Why it earns its place

Every bounce hurts your Gmail reputation (P4), and today we only find out AFTER sending. A DNS MX lookup — does this domain even receive mail? — is free, takes ~50ms, and catches the most common real-world failure: typos like @gamil.com or a dead startup's domain.

Scope guard

  • At add-contact: MX lookup on the domain, non-blocking amber warning ("this domain can't receive mail — check for a typo") with override
  • NOT SMTP-probing mailboxes (that's what gets IPs blacklisted) — domain-level only
  • ~40 lines backend + reuse the existing cooldown-style warning UI
Verdict: highest bounce-prevention per line of code available to us. BUILD.
Study 04 · from similar tools

Template health check (deliverability lint)

Mail-tester's idea, as instant client-side hints in the template editor.
BUILD-LITE

What it checks (all local, no API)

  • Spam-trigger words ("free money", "act now", excessive "!!!")
  • Subject length (30-60 chars sweet spot), body length, link count
  • ALL-CAPS ratio, missing {signature}

Honest limits

Heuristics, not SpamAssassin — it nudges toward better copy, it can't promise inbox placement. Ship as quiet hints under the editor (like the variable chips), never a blocking gate.

Verdict: cheap, on-principle (P4), genuinely educational for the learning-repo goal (P6). BUILD-LITE — worth one small pass, not more.
Study 05 · considered and parked / rejected

The rest, quickly

Contact timeline drawer — PARK

Streak-style per-contact history (every send, reply, label in one panel). Real value once you're juggling 50+ threads, but the Pipeline board just shipped and covers most of it. Re-evaluate when thread volume actually hurts.

Warm-up networks & open tracking — REJECT, permanently

Instantly/Lemlist sell inbox "warm-up" (bot networks sending fake engagement) and open-tracking pixels. Both violate P4 (Google actively bans warm-up networks) and P5 (surveillance). These stay in the never column with DM automation.

Net result of this exploration: 4 green-lit candidates — Open-in-Gmail deep links, {booking_link} integration, MX verification, template health check. Per our workflow, whichever you approve gets mocked in docs/artifact.html first, then built.
